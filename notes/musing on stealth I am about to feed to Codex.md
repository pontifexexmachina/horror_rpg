Okay, so. Here's a quote from Sean McCoy, the designer of Mothership, as to why that game doesn't have a Stealth skill of some sort:

```
A big thing that we didn’t design for in Mothership was stealth mechanics. But the game is also about running and hiding. We don’t have fleeing rules either. Here’s why….

What I mean is that it was the one part of the game I didn’t want you to be able to skip over with a roll. I wanted this moment:

“I want to hide".
“Okay where are you hiding?”
“Shit what’s around me?”
“Gear lockers, surgical bed, ventilation shaft.”
”Shit.”
”It’s coming”

“Okay the lockers”
”Okay you won’t fit with your vacsuit on. Do you want to take it off?”
”No. I need the armor. The ventilation shaft.”
”Alright you’ll have to unscrew it.”
”Is there time?”
”You can make a speed check.”
”No fuck it. I’m hiding under the bed.”

Hiding is important. So important that I want all that tension. All those question and answer sessions that make these games so fun to me. Think about where you’re hiding. No, “my stealth is good so I find a place.” Really think.
```

That's all well and good, but that doesn't teach either players or GMs how to actually do that. And my experience in actually running Mothership is that what happens is either:

* Players talk the GM into making the ill-defined "Combat Training" skill the "god skill" that covers most of the game's skill gaps based around this philosophy, or
* Players flounder without a structure to use to tell them what is/isn't possible

So let's talk through some aspects of stealth and related things in horror games. You have:

## Kinds of stealth

One kind is what I'm going to call Alien: Isolation stealth. Hiding from raptors in the kitchen in Jurassic Park stealth. This is where you're hiding from the monster and hoping it goes away. This could be to save resources, because you're out of ammo, because someone is low on health, because you're outmatched for a certain monster at this point.

The other is ambush stealth, where you know an enemy is there, they don't, and you're trying to get the drop on them.

They should obviously be related, but I think there are important differences between them, and I think that games with less of a stealth/sneaking emphasis can tend to boil them down to the same mechanic, when if you add more depth they can distinguish themselves a bit.

## Sound

In Resident Evil games, you can't just break down a door instead of finding the right key or wirecutter or whatever. The play culture for TTRPGs is such that you can't just keep people from breaking down doors by not putting a break down door button in the game.

But breaking down doors is louder than using keys. Shooting guns makes noise. Noise in dark, quiet places attracts attention.

This shouldn't be an automatic fail state, though, and there should be a player-facing way to tell how risky noise is at any time. This helps build a rhythm and build tension. The more noise you create, the more risk you should be creating, though.

## Light

Light is a huge part of these kinds of games. Light helps you see, which is good. But also light tells everyone else you're there, which is not good.

More than that -- light vs dark is a big part of how these games build tension and narrative. The extreme example is Alan Wake, where the flashlight can be used to stun enemies and render invulnerable enemies vulnerable. Moira's flashlight in Resident Evil Revelations 2 also has similar gameplay -- stun enemies, reveal hidden objects, etc. But these extra uses of flashlights burn down batteries faster. (Which plays into the resource consumption paradigm of the game.)

But there's also just a reason why the first survival horror game is called Alone In The Dark. Cutting through the dark with light is an essential part of the iconography of these games -- especially when the light only cuts through part of the dark.

So light ties into:

* Stealth
* Combat
* Resources
* Exploration
* Narrative

This game can probably afford to have the most involved light mechanics of any TTRPG because it ties into _everything_.

(I don't know if more involved makes them better, I just think we should be thoughtful about how we use light and make it feel like an important part of the game.)

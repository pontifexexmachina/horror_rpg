# TTRPG Layout, Graphic Design, and Usability Reading Report

## Framing

Your first list was already strong on **scenario structure**, **procedural play**, and **mechanics that help or obstruct actual table action**. What it underweighted was the part of TTRPG design that decides whether anyone will actually *learn* the thing, *use* the thing, or even *pick it up in the first place*.

That missing layer is not decoration. In TTRPGs, layout and graphic design do at least four jobs at once:

1. **Sell tone** before the reader has processed a single rule.
2. **Teach the game** while the reader is still deciding whether to commit.
3. **Support reference at the table** when no one has time for your lovingly typeset wall of prose.
4. **Shape the physical workflow of play** through sheets, pamphlets, cards, maps, screen inserts, bookmarks, hyperlinks, indexes, and page architecture.

A rulebook that looks cool but teaches badly is a poster that wandered into the wrong industry.

This pass is therefore focused on the strongest resources and case studies for:

- character sheets as teaching tools
- rulebooks as both teaching texts and reference texts
- visual signaling and navigation
- accessibility and multiple output formats
- compact/high-density formats (zines, pamphlets, digest books)
- covers and art direction as part of the product, not afterthoughts
- the practical boring stuff that is secretly not boring at all: indexes, bookmarks, hyperlinks, print ergonomics

## Executive summary

### The big takeaways

- **Mothership is still the center of gravity for this shelf.** Sean McCoy’s character-sheet essay is one of the cleanest statements of “the sheet should teach the game.” Pair that with the broader Mothership interviews and you get a coherent philosophy: compact format, teachable procedures, low-prep reference, and a very deliberate relationship between the visual object and the imagined world.
- **The single best “general principle” resource is still the Brennen Reece interview at Bully Pulpit.** Not because it is long or academic, but because it says the quiet part out loud: RPG books are **teaching texts first and reference texts second**. That one sentence is an axe through a lot of hobby nonsense.
- **You need a real accessibility shelf.** Accessible Games and Psychhound are not side dishes. They cover the practical work too many RPG books still duck: PDF layers, bookmarks, structure, chapter summaries, low-ink options, plain text, large print, alt text, and release-format thinking.
- **Explorers Design is currently one of the better living sources for layout/usability thinking inside the hobby.** It is especially good on character sheets, visual signals, accessibility, and cover design.
- **Use the ENNIE Best Layout & Design category as radar, not gospel.** It is good at finding ambitious or polished books to study. It is not a perfect filter for “best at teaching” versus “best at looking expensive and/or metal as hell.”
- **Do not let people memory-hole D&D here either.** Mentzer Red Box deserves study as a self-teaching game text; modern D&D still explicitly invests in onboarding through beginner sheets, free rules, and reorganized/streamlined learning paths.

### The short version of what you were missing

You had critique and systems thinking. You were lighter on:

- typography and page architecture
- book usability and navigation
- character-sheet craft
- accessibility
- cover design and product signaling
- component/format design
- onboarding design, especially in mainstream products

## What I would add to your shelf immediately

## Tier 1: Must-read TTRPG-specific resources

These are the things I would hand to a TTRPG designer who wants to get serious about layout, usability, and teaching.

### 1. Sean McCoy, **Lessons I Learned Designing the Mothership Character Sheet**
- Link: <https://www.failuretolerated.com/lessons-i-learned-designing-the-mothership-character-sheet>
- Why it matters: This is the strongest single piece on **the character sheet as tutorial**. McCoy is explicitly asking how much of character creation and play can be taught from the sheet itself.
- What to steal:
  - “Start here” thinking
  - directional flow on the sheet
  - sheet as onboarding, not recap
  - visual style that supports genre rather than just decorating it

### 2. Bully Pulpit Games, **Grimoires & Garamond: Brennen Reece on RPG Book Design**
- Link: <https://bullypulpitgames.com/blogs/news/grimoires-garamond-brennen-reece-on-rpg-book-design>
- Why it matters: Probably the best short essay/interview on the shelf for correcting hobby-brain. Reece calls RPGs **teaching texts first and reference texts second**, praises the **1983 Mentzer Red Box** as a self-teaching text, and recommends looking beyond RPGs to instructional books in other domains.
- What to steal:
  - your job is communication first
  - typography is not ornamental frosting
  - borrow from cookbooks, craft books, field manuals, textbooks, not just other RPGs

### 3. Accessible Games, **Accessible Guide to RPG Layout**
- Link: <https://www.accessiblegames.biz/our-games/accessible-guide-rpg-layout/>
- Why it matters: One of the few actual process-oriented layout resources written for RPG creators. It covers the trip from manuscript to published product with accessibility baked in.
- What to steal:
  - layered PDFs
  - tagged/bookmarked PDFs
  - workflow discipline before you get deep into page makeup
  - publishing practices that don’t make your digital version actively annoying

### 4. Luka Brave / Psychhound Games, **The Accessible Rulebook Checklist**
- Link: <https://psychhound.com/2025/10/17/accessible-rulebook-checklist/>
- Why it matters: Practical, recent, and extremely usable. It addresses “cognitive frontload” head-on.
- What to steal:
  - chapter overviews and summaries
  - build-along examples
  - short chapters with obvious purpose
  - grayscale readability
  - line spacing / tracking discipline
  - quick-reference handouts
  - planning accessibility before release, not apologizing after release

### 5. Explorers Design, **Turn the Character Sheet Sideways**
- Link: <https://www.explorersdesign.com/turn-the-sheet-sideways/>
- Why it matters: Excellent concise argument for the character sheet as the compressed essence of the game.
- What to steal:
  - the idea that the sheet is a **distillation**
  - thinking about landscape orientation instead of defaulting to portrait because everyone else does
  - removing anything a Post-it could do better
  - using spacing and grouping to create breathing room and priority

### 6. Explorers Design, **How to Design Signals in Books**
- Link: <https://www.explorersdesign.com/designing-signals/>
- Why it matters: Under-discussed but crucial. A lot of RPG books are stuffed with repeated decorative elements that accidentally imply meaning. This article talks about **signals**—visual patterns that tell the reader what matters and how to navigate.
- What to steal:
  - avoid false signals
  - repeat only meaningful patterns
  - align TOC language, running headers, and body signals
  - treat hyperlinks and physical affordances as part of navigation design

### 7. Explorers Design, **Accessibility in Tabletop Design**
- Link: <https://www.explorersdesign.com/accessibility-design/>
- Why it matters: Good corrective to the idea that accessibility is one checkbox. It treats accessibility as multiple products and multiple audiences.
- What to steal:
  - versioning the game (PDF, audio, text-only, etc.)
  - designing for actual assistive tech use
  - treating accessibility like a release gate

### 8. Off Shelf, **Mothership’s Sean McCoy**
- Link: <https://offshelf.net/2020/04/27/motherships-sean-mccoy/>
- Why it matters: Helpful complement to the character sheet article. McCoy talks about zine format, hackability, theme-first design, and writing room keys that can be parsed quickly at the table.
- What to steal:
  - compact format as constraint, not gimmick
  - prep-light reference writing
  - designing presentation around actual table use

### 9. Off Shelf, **Mork Borg and Putrescence Regnant’s Johan Nohr**
- Link: <https://offshelf.net/2021/03/18/mork-borg-and-putrescence-regnants-johan-nohr/>
- Why it matters: Crucial if you want to understand **why MÖRK BORG works instead of dismissing it as “just vibes.”** Nohr is very explicit that the project wanted to look loud, scream at the reader, and behave more like a poster/zine object than a traditional sober rulebook.
- What to steal:
  - typography as illustration
  - when chaos is actually doing thematic work
  - the trick of contrasting high-chaos pages with **hyper-utilitarian reference sections**
  - thinking about non-book formats and how they actually sit on a table

### 10. Mottokrosh Machinations interviews and posts
- Start here:
  - **Interview with Dan Algstrand**: <https://mottokrosh.com/posts/2020/03/interview-with-dan-algstrand/>
  - **Interview with Pelle Nilsson and Johan Nohr**: <https://mottokrosh.com/posts/2020/03/interview-with-pelle-nilsson-and-johan-nohr/>
  - **Looming Machinations**: <https://mottokrosh.com/posts/2020/02/looming-machinations/>
- Why they matter: These are not formal treatises, but they are very good process windows. Algstrand is especially good on the division between **graphic designer** and **layout person**, and on the misery and importance of indexing. The Hypertellurians revision note is great because it states the goal plainly: improve **ease of comprehension** and **utility at the table**.
- What to steal:
  - treat indexing as design, not cleanup
  - separate surface identity from information placement
  - revise for comprehension, not just for more content

## Tier 2: Non-RPG books you should absolutely steal from

This is the other big gap. The hobby too often reads only itself, then wonders why its books behave like over-decorated spell scrolls.

### 1. Robert Bringhurst, **The Elements of Typographic Style**
- Why it matters: This is the classic. Brennen Reece name-checks it for a reason.
- What it gives you: type selection, hierarchy, rhythm, spacing, proportion, and the basic conviction that typography exists to honor the text instead of strangling it.

### 2. Ellen Lupton, **Thinking with Type**
- Link: <https://ellenlupton.com/Thinking-with-Type>
- Why it matters: One of the best general introductions to type, hierarchy, grids, and composition for people who are smart but not formally trained.
- What it gives you: a working vocabulary and visual literacy.

### 3. Robin Williams, **The Non-Designer’s Design Book**
- Link: <https://www.pearson.com/en-us/subject-catalog/p/non-designers-design-book-the/P200000000691/9780137461370>
- Why it matters: If you need the practical “stop making avoidable mistakes” version.
- What it gives you: contrast, repetition, alignment, proximity, and a clean beginner grammar.

### 4. Steve Krug, **Don’t Make Me Think**
- Link: <https://sensible.com/dont-make-me-think/>
- Why it matters: It is a usability book, nominally for web and software, but the mindset ports perfectly to RPG books.
- What it gives you: ruthless clarity about navigation, expectation, and user confusion.

### 5. Ginny Redish, **Letting Go of the Words**
- Link: <https://redish.net/books/letting-go-of-the-words/>
- Why it matters: Tremendously relevant if you think of a rulebook as a user-facing instructional system.
- What it gives you: chunking, plain language, organization, and writing for people who are trying to *do something*, not admire your prose.

### 6. Josef Müller-Brockmann, **Grid Systems in Graphic Design**
- Link: <https://draw-down.com/products/grid-systems-in-graphic-design>
- Why it matters: The anti-chaos counterweight. Even if you later break the grid, you should know what you are breaking.
- What it gives you: structure, consistency, and a framework for solving page problems instead of improvising them badly.

## The real gaps in your current shelf

If I were blunt about it, your earlier shelf risked overfitting to “mechanics and scenario design” and underfitting to “the book as a machine for learning and play.” The big missing categories were:

### 1. Character-sheet design
This is not production work. It is game design. The sheet is often the most interacted-with artifact in play.

### 2. Navigation design
TOCs, running heads, repeated section structures, indexes, margin cues, tabs, internal hyperlinks, PDF bookmarks. People pretend these are boring because they do not have a metal logo attached.

### 3. Accessibility
Not just font choice. Also chaptering, summaries, examples, low-ink versions, text-only versions, large print, alt text, layers, tags, and assistive-technology compatibility.

### 4. Cover and signal design
A cover is not “the art on the front.” It is the first piece of rules explanation your audience receives. It tells them what fantasy they are buying into and what emotional channel the game occupies.

### 5. Format ergonomics
Zines, digest books, pamphlets, cards, gatefolds, playbooks, boxed sets, inventory cards, map inserts. The physical/digital object changes how the game gets learned and run.

### 6. Mainstream onboarding craft
This is the piece most “cool indie lists” underplay. D&D absolutely belongs in this conversation when discussing onboarding, learning flow, starter products, and sheet design—even when it is not the most visually radical example.

## The best case studies to study by example

This is the fun part.

## A. Best examples of **character sheet as tutorial**

### Mothership
- Start here because you already know why.
- Study the sheet next to the article, not separately.
- Look for:
  - where the eye goes first
  - how the arrows and flow imply action order
  - how much character creation is learnable from the page itself
  - where genre styling helps versus where it risks clutter

### Blades in the Dark playbooks and player kit
- Downloads: <https://bladesinthedark.com/downloads>
- Why it matters: Blades turned the sheet/playbook into a **delivery vehicle for procedures**. Its one-page playsheets are explicitly aimed at new players and GMs learning the game.
- What to study:
  - specific rules attached to specific playbooks
  - procedures compressed into player-facing material
  - large-print support as a concrete accessibility move

### Mausritter inventory cards and slots
- Official site: <https://mausritter.com/>
- Why it matters: One of the best examples of using **components** to teach and constrain play. Physical item cards make inventory management tactile and legible.
- What to study:
  - how the component itself enforces the rule
  - how tactile interaction reduces explanation overhead

### D&D 5E Character Sheets (especially the intro sheet)
- Official product page: <https://wpn.wizards.com/en/products/dandd-character-sheets>
- Why it matters: Worth studying precisely because it is mainstream and trying to reduce intimidation. Wizards explicitly sold this pack with an **introductory character sheet designed to ease new players into the game**.
- What to study:
  - progressive disclosure for novice players
  - variants for veteran preference versus beginner guidance

## B. Best examples of **rulebook as teaching text**

### Mentzer Red Box (1983)
- Not included here as a direct file link for obvious reasons, but it is the D&D example that should stay in the canon.
- Why it matters: Brennen Reece specifically points to it as a mass-market self-teaching success.
- What to study:
  - how it stages learning
  - what it refuses to frontload
  - how its tone supports confidence instead of showing off

### Shadowdark Quickstart Set
- Official page: <https://www.thearcanelibrary.com/products/shadowdark-rpg-quickstart-set-pdf>
- Why it matters: Two digest-sized booklets, pregens with art/stat split, and a deliberately compact starter format. Good modern example of learning-oriented packaging.
- What to study:
  - how much fits in a digest format without claustrophobia
  - division between player and GM booklets
  - starter-set ergonomics rather than single-monolith-book thinking

### Modern D&D onboarding material
- 2024 Free Rules: <https://www.dndbeyond.com/posts/1804-start-playing-today-with-the-2024-d-d-free-rules>
- 2024 PHB character creation article: <https://www.dndbeyond.com/posts/1787-how-to-create-a-character-using-the-2024-players>
- 2024 DMG onboarding article: <https://www.dndbeyond.com/posts/1829-how-the-2024-dungeon-masters-guide-makes-it-easier>
- Why it matters: Even if these are marketing-adjacent, they still show what the publisher thinks matters for onboarding: **streamlining**, **rearrangement for learning**, **free entry points**, and **stepwise guidance**.
- What to study:
  - the language of reassurance and approachability
  - what gets moved earlier in the learning sequence
  - how a big mainstream game tries to de-friction first contact

## C. Best examples of **loud aesthetic that still manages utility**

### MÖRK BORG
- Start with the Off Shelf interview and the Mottokrosh interview.
- Why it matters: The obvious trap is to reduce it to “punk visual overload.” The better reading is that it is a case study in **intentional noise**—and in strategically using moments of severe clarity where reference needs are highest.
- What to study:
  - where chaos carries tone versus where it would wreck usability
  - the contrast between poster spreads and utilitarian sections
  - how far typography can become image before it stops functioning

### Putrescence Regnant
- Why it matters: Strong example of **book-object / package-object** thinking. Gameable content appears on sleeve, cover, and booklet, not just in a single interior block.
- What to study:
  - how the object distributes information across surfaces
  - how table use changes the best unit of layout
  - the value of “single-page usable states” for awkward formats

### CBR+PNK
- Official page: <https://emanoelmelo.itch.io/cbrpnk-core>
- Plain-text rules: <https://cbrpnk.cabinetofcuriosities.games/>
- Why it matters: One of the clearest modern examples of **format as design argument**. The game is presented as a series of eight-panel pamphlets, and there is also a plain-text rules site for reference.
- What to study:
  - information partitioning by pamphlet
  - player-facing vs GM-facing split
  - how alternative delivery formats broaden usability

## D. Best examples of **clean premium modern bookcraft**

### Free League / Christian Granath / Dan Algstrand orbit
Useful places to start:
- Blade Runner RPG Core Rulebook (2023 ENNIE Gold, Best Layout & Design)
- ALIEN RPG
- Vaesen
- Forbidden Lands
- Symbaroum / Symbaroum Monster Codex

Why it matters:
- Free League keeps showing up because it is very good at combining atmosphere, readability, and product confidence.
- The Mottokrosh Algstrand interview is especially useful because it clarifies the division between overall graphic identity and layout execution.

What to study:
- disciplined hierarchy
- how art is given room without starving the text
- premium-feeling layout that still points the reader somewhere

### Mythic Bastionland
- Why it matters: 2025 ENNIE Gold in Best Layout & Design makes it an obvious current study target.
- What to study:
  - how modern indie fantasy presentation can feel authored without becoming visually self-indulgent

### Heart: The City Beneath
- Why it matters: 2021 ENNIE Gold in Best Layout & Design. One of the strongest “whole product” examples from the last several years.
- What to study:
  - how a heavy-setting, heavy-tone book still preserves navigability
  - how visual identity and content architecture support each other

### Delta Green: Impossible Landscapes
- Why it matters: 2022 ENNIE Gold in Best Layout & Design. Particularly useful as a case study in handling a dense, atmospheric, scenario-rich text without flattening it.
- What to study:
  - chapter rhythm
  - use of graphic language to control mood and orientation
  - what “premium horror book” looks like when it still needs to be run

## E. Best examples of **compact / modular information design**

### BREAK!!
- Why it matters: 2024 ENNIE Best Layout & Design nominee, and worth examining as a modern friendly/approachable fantasy presentation.
- What to study:
  - how it packages approachability without flattening personality
  - how rules and descriptive material are paired

### TEETH
- Why it matters: 2024 nominee and a strong candidate for studying whether a book can be sharply styled without hiding its own procedures.

### Shadowdark
- Why it matters: 2024 nominee with a highly teachable starter package and digest-based presentation.

### Hypertellurians (especially the revised-edition thinking)
- Why it matters: The Mottokrosh post matters more than hype here. The explicit goals—**ease of comprehension** and **utility at the table**—are exactly the right goals.

## What this pass suggests you should now care about most

If I were translating all this into design priorities, I would say the hobby’s best recent work cares about the following five questions:

### 1. Can the game teach itself?
Not fully, obviously—but more than most books try to do.

Look at:
- Mothership sheet
- Blades playbooks
- beginner D&D materials
- Shadowdark quickstart

### 2. Can the book be skimmed without being gutted?
A lot of RPG books are readable only front-to-back, which is useless once play begins.

Look at:
- chapter architecture
- recurring section formats
- summaries
- callouts
- margin notes
- running heads
- cross-references

### 3. Are the signals honest?
If two boxes look the same, do they mean the same thing? If a flourish repeats, does it communicate anything? Decorative sameness is one of the hobby’s favorite sins.

Look at:
- Explorers Design on signals
- Bringhurst / Lupton on hierarchy

### 4. Does the digital version respect the reader?
You can have gorgeous print design and a garbage PDF. Sadly, this is common.

Look at:
- bookmarks
- hyperlinks
- selectable text
- layers
- low-ink variants
- plain-text or web-text alternatives

### 5. Does the physical format improve play, or just posture as clever?
A weird form factor is not automatically good. It needs to earn its weirdness.

Look at:
- pamphlets (CBR+PNK)
- zines (Mothership)
- sleeves/gatefolds/single-page states (Putrescence Regnant)
- cards and slot systems (Mausritter)

## The strongest principles I’d steal from all of this

These are the opinions I would keep taped above the monitor:

1. **The character sheet is not bookkeeping. It is game design in compression.**
2. **A rulebook should be teachable in chunks and searchable in panic.**
3. **If a repeated visual element does not signal meaning, it is probably noise.**
4. **Accessibility is not charity. It is product competence.**
5. **Indexes, bookmarks, hyperlinks, summaries, and examples are not “production details.” They are core UX.**
6. **A cover should make the right people want to open the book, not vaguely imply fantasy product inside.**
7. **Most RPG books need fewer clever fonts and more hierarchy.**
8. **Looking at only RPG books is how RPG layout stays trapped inside the same bad habits.**
9. **A beautiful book that can’t be used at the table has failed half its job.**
10. **Mainstream examples should not be excluded just because they are mainstream.** D&D, especially in onboarding and beginner-facing design, still belongs on the shelf.

## A very practical audit checklist for any TTRPG book or sheet

Use this when studying any of the examples above.

### Teaching / onboarding
- Can a new player understand what the game is *about* from the cover and first spread?
- Can they create a character without reading the entire book first?
- Is there a clear start-here path?
- Are examples placed next to the rules they explain?
- Are there chapter overviews and end-of-section summaries?

### Navigation / reference
- Do running headers actually help?
- Is the table of contents specific enough to be useful?
- Is there an index, and is it good?
- Are cross-references clickable in PDF?
- Do repeated layouts establish reliable expectations?

### Page craft
- Is hierarchy obvious at a glance?
- Is there enough white space, or is every page trying to win a shouting contest?
- Are sidebars useful, or just decorative parking lots?
- Can the page survive grayscale or cheap printing?
- Are important procedures broken into visible steps?

### Character sheet / player-facing tools
- Does the sheet tell you what matters in play?
- Does it foreground incomplete things the player should care about?
- Does it reduce page flipping?
- Does it teach procedure order?
- Does it suggest tone without obscuring function?

### Accessibility / digital competence
- Is the text selectable?
- Is the PDF bookmarked?
- Are there hyperlinks?
- Is there a low-ink or plain-text option?
- Has the designer thought about large print, audio, alt text, or assistive technology?

### Format / physical use
- Does the object sit on the table well?
- Can players and GM each have the information they need without wrestling the same book?
- Is the chosen trim size helping, neutral, or actively making things worse?
- Are components teaching rules through their form?

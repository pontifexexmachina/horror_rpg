
# TTRPG design reading map for your shelf

Prepared April 6, 2026.

## What your current list is *really* about

Your list is not random. It has a strong center of gravity:

- **Scenario architecture and procedure** over vague “storytelling advice”
- **Information design**: clues, legwork, mystery structure, player-facing discovery
- **GM-facing usability**: what the GM can actually prep, run, and improvise at the table
- **Critiques of abstraction**: dissociated mechanics, hard limits, rules that block obvious fictional action
- **A bias toward procedures that produce play**, not just opinions about play
- **Some UX / presentation awareness**, via the Mothership character-sheet piece

That is a very healthy shelf. It means you are already reading better material than most “how to design an RPG” lists.

Where it is thinner:

- **Reward loops / incentive design / metacurrency**
- **Consequence architecture and partial success design**
- **Pacing / dramatic beat structure**
- **Probability, scaling, and math philosophy**
- **Long-campaign operating models** beyond sandbox/scenario procedure
- **Playtesting, editing, layout, and accessibility**
- **Primary-source D&D design writing** from the people who actually shipped 3E/5E-era ideas that everybody copied

## 3) The D&D / d20 shelf you explicitly asked not to memory-hole

## This shelf matters a lot

People get weirdly hipster about D&D design writing, which is dumb. Whether or not you like the finished games, **3E and 5E shipped some of the most imitated design ideas in the hobby**. Ignoring the designers’ own explanation of those choices is self-inflicted ignorance.

### Rodney Thompson — Bounded Accuracy
<https://web.archive.org/web/20140715051206/http://www.wizards.com/dnd/article.aspx?x=dnd/4ll/20120604>

This is one of the most important modern d20 design essays, full stop. Even if you hate 5E, you should read the article where one of its key structural ideas is explained in the open.

### Mike Mearls — Playtest: First Round Overview
<https://web.archive.org/web/20120530193547/http://www.wizards.com:80/DnD/Article.aspx?x=dnd/4ll/20120528>

This is especially worth reading because it catches design thinking **before** the game calcified into finished-product rhetoric. It talks about themes, old-school mode, character-sheet presentation, skill reliability, and — crucially — advantage/disadvantage while it was still just emerging.

### Mike Mearls — Design Finesse—Part 1
<https://web.archive.org/web/20131127070333/http://www.wizards.com:80/dnd/Article.aspx?x=dnd/4ll/20131125>

If you only read one Mearls design piece, read this. It’s blunt about elegance, simplification, and removing problems by removing rules. It also directly explains advantage/disadvantage as a way to eat giant piles of fiddly modifiers.

### Nekohaus D&D archive index
<https://blog.nekohaus.net/?category=DnD>

This is not “the resource,” but it is a very good **map to missing Wizards-era design writing** that got half-swallowed by the web, including:
- *Playtest: First Round Overview*
- *The Advantage of Rolling Dice*
- *Reacting to the Reaction*
- *D&D Next Goals, Part One–Four*
- *Design Finesse—Part 1/2*
- assorted 2012–2014 Legends & Lore pieces

If the original link 404s, Nekohaus is often the breadcrumb trail that gets you back to Wayback.

### Monte Cook — Table Solutions
<https://www.montecookgames.com/table-solutions/>

This is Cypher-specific in the surface details, but the real value is the framing of how much of a ruleset is covered by explicit procedure versus by GM judgement.

### Monte Cook — Your Best Game Ever
<https://www.montecookgames.com/store/product/your-best-game-ever/>

This is broader than “design,” but broad in a useful way: table operations, player/GM problems, online play, publishing-adjacent concerns, and a lot of practical craft. Worth reading partly because Monte is a 3E co-designer and partly because the contributor list is stacked.

### Monte Cook — Don’t Randomize Fun
<https://www.montecookgames.com/mcg-episodes/dont-randomize-fun-monte-cooks-advice-for-your-best-game-ever/>

Good corrective if you want to think clearly about where randomness is helping a game and where it is just clogging the pipes.

### Consent in Gaming
<https://www.montecookgames.com/consent-in-gaming/>

Not “mechanical design,” but absolutely part of modern RPG product design and table design.

## The biggest gaps in your current shelf

## 1) Reward economies and incentives

Your list is excellent at “how to structure a scenario” and “what kinds of mechanics are bad or dissociated,” but it is thinner on **what the game rewards and how those rewards steer behavior**.

Read for that:
- Fate (*Aspects & Fate Points*, *Fate Point Economy*, *Bronze Rule*)
- Burning Wheel
- PbtA essays
- Blades in the Dark

## 2) Consequence design

You have a lot of material on clues, legwork, and proceduralism. You have less on **what happens when rolls go mixed, partial, costly, or sideways**.

Read for that:
- *Setting Position & Effect*
- *Progress Clocks*
- Burning Wheel’s “failure leads to the next conflict” ethos

## 3) Pacing

A lot of RPG design advice is good at structure and bad at rhythm. Your shelf could use more about **tension waves, reversals, and emotional timing**.

Read:
- *Hamlet’s Hit Points*
- *Beating the Story*

## 4) Math and scaling

You are already reading people who care about procedures, but not much of your shelf says, “Okay, what does this mechanic *do probabilistically*, and what campaign assumptions does that math imply?”

Read:
- *Bounded Accuracy*
- early advantage/disadvantage material
- contemporary math breakdowns of advantage/disadvantage if you want the numeric side

## 5) Playtesting and revision craft

A lot of RPG discourse acts like the important thing is Having Opinions. It isn’t. The important thing is **getting the game to the table, reading what broke, and changing the right thing instead of the loudest thing**.

Read:
- *Powered by the Apocalypse, Part 1*
- *The Gentle Art of Playtest Feedback Wrangling*
- *Design Finesse—Part 1*

## 6) Product usability

You already have the Mothership character-sheet piece, which is a good sign. But the shelf could go much harder on:
- layout
- accessibility
- editing
- presentation
- learnability

Read:
- *A Beginner’s Guide to Affordable Layout*
- *Accessible Guide to RPG Layout*
- Monte’s character-sheet comments in *Playtest: First Round Overview*

## My strong-opinion “canon” for TTRPG designers

If I had to build a **12-item canon** that covers the major design families without pretending D&D never happened, it would be this:

1. **Rodney Thompson — Bounded Accuracy**  
   Because modern d20 math/scaling assumptions matter, and this is one of the clearest primary-source statements of them.

2. **Mike Mearls — Design Finesse—Part 1**  
   Because “remove the problem instead of adding a patch” is one of the few universally useful RPG design lessons.

3. **Mike Mearls — Playtest: First Round Overview**  
   Because early-stage design writing is often more revealing than polished post-launch explanation.

4. **The Alexandrian — Three Clue Rule**  
   Because most mystery/adventure design still screws this up.

5. **The Alexandrian — Node-Based Scenario Design**  
   Because flexible scenario topology matters.

6. **The Alexandrian — Game Structures**  
   Because lots of designers don’t even realize they are missing a structure problem.

7. **Pelgrane / Robin D. Laws — Give a Clue + Clues vs Leads + Investigative Abilities as Scenario Springboards**  
   Count this as one cluster. It is among the best scenario-design writing in the hobby.

8. **Vincent Baker — Powered by the Apocalypse, Part 1**  
   Because it’s one of the best explicit statements that design is iterative and should become playable fast.

9. **Blades in the Dark SRD — Position & Effect + Progress Clocks**  
   Count this as one cluster. Hugely important for consequence design.

10. **Fate Core / Toolkit — Aspects, Fate Point Economy, Bronze Rule**  
    Count this as one cluster. Important for incentive design and flexible modeling.

11. **Burning Wheel**  
    Because belief-driven reward structures and “failure leads somewhere” are too important to ignore.

12. **Hamlet’s Hit Points**  
    Because scenario logic without pacing produces flat play.

If you want a **13th item**, make it **Complete Kobold Guide to Game Design, 2e** as the broad practical anthology.

## Bottom line

Your current list is already smarter than the average “RPG design resources” bundle, because it cares about **usable procedures** instead of just ideology. The biggest upgrade now is not “more OSR essays.” It is to add the missing shelves:

- **D&D primary-source design writing** from the 3E/5E orbit
- **reward-loop design** (Fate, Burning Wheel)
- **consequence architecture** (Blades / FitD)
- **pacing** (Hamlet’s Hit Points)
- **playtesting / revision craft**
- **layout and accessibility**

That gives you a much more complete picture of what RPG design actually is.
